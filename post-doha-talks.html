Doha Tripartite Talks: Iran and Qatar Discuss Implementation Details of Ceasefire Agreement

July 1, 2026

International Affairs Team

This week, Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister led a delegation consisting of officials from the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, the Central Bank, and other departments to Doha, Qatar, where they met with Qatar's Prime Minister and Minister of Foreign Affairs. The core topic of discussion was how to implement the previously reached US-Iran memorandum of understanding, including attempts to "clear obstacles" for the next stage of negotiations on specific issues like implementing ceasefire arrangements in Lebanon.

Following the meeting, representatives from Iran, Qatar, and Pakistan held a follow-up tripartite meeting in Doha specifically to review the current execution progress of the US-Iran memorandum of understanding. The Iranian side revealed that a dedicated working group has been established to follow up on the implementation of the memorandum and prepare for negotiations on a final agreement. However, formal negotiations have not yet commenced, and the exact time and location are still being coordinated through intermediaries, to be launched once conditions are ripe.

Almost simultaneously, the US President stated publicly that the US and Iran are currently "doing well," that recent talks in Qatar progressed smoothly, and noted that Iran's denuclearization process is moving forward steadily. This stance forms an interesting contrast with the relatively cautious wording from Iranian officials—one side offers optimistic public statements while the other involves actual work to detail implementation plans. This divergence in pacing precisely reflects the complexity of the easing process in the Middle East.

Observers pointed out that Doha, as a traditional hub for Middle Eastern diplomatic mediation, has once again assumed the role of a facilitator. Regardless of whether final negotiations can start as scheduled, this round of shuttle diplomacy demonstrates at least that all parties remain willing to sit at the negotiating table, which in itself is a positive signal that the situation has not deteriorated further.
